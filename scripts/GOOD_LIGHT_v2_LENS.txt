iGOOD LIGHT — VERSION 2 (cold open: THE LENS)

a short film  ·  ~3 minutes  ·  9:16 or 16:9


FADE IN:


EXT. A SHOPFRONT ACROSS THE STREET — MORNING

THROUGH A LONG LENS. Handheld, voyeuristic. The frame hunts across a
busy pavement — racks focus — and finds MEI (40s, apron) inside a
small café, alone behind her counter. Slow breathing, close to the
mic. The frame holds on her like a target.

The lens tilts down to the café doorway. A YOUNG COUPLE slows at the
chalkboard menu propped outside — handwritten, no pictures. They read
it. They glance into the empty room. They lose interest — and walk on,
toward a glossy chain café down the street.

A man's voice, quiet, to himself:

                    CHOON (O.S.)
          ...every time.

PULL BACK — it's CHOON (40s), at the window of a shop across the
street, lowering a camera from his eye. Not a threat. A regular,
watching a place he loves go under. He frowns at the drifting crowd,
then slides the camera into his bag.


EXT. STREET — CONTINUOUS

Choon crosses the road toward the café, weaving through the people who
won't.


INT. SECONDS — A SMALL NEIGHBOURHOOD CAFÉ — CONTINUOUS

The door chimes. Choon steps in. MEI brightens despite herself.

                    CHOON
          Morning, Mei. The usual.

                    MEI
          (already moving to the machine)
          Sit, sit.

She pulls a shot. We watch her care — the timing, the pour, the
micro-foam folding into the cup. She sets it down: a flat white, a
clean tulip resting in the crema.

Choon sips. Closes his eyes a moment.

                    CHOON
          Best coffee on this street. It's not
          even close.

                    MEI
          (a small, sad smile)
          Tell the landlord.

                    CHOON
          ...What?

She doesn't answer right away. Wipes the counter. By the register: an
opened LETTER — "...rental revision..." — a figure circled in red.

                    MEI
          End of the month. Maybe next. Rent went
          up. People walk past. They don't come in.

Choon glances back toward the door, the street beyond it.

                    CHOON
          (quietly)
          I watched three people read that board
          this morning. Not one of them came in.

                    MEI
          Every day.

Choon leans to see the board from the inside. Faded handwriting.
Words and prices. No pictures.

                    CHOON
          Mei — can I try something? No charge.
          It'll bother me if I don't.

                    MEI
          Try what?

He's already lifting the camera back out of the bag.

                    CHOON
          Make me one more. Your best one. Don't
          rush it.

                                                  CUT TO:


INT. SECONDS — BY THE WINDOW — MOMENTS LATER

Choon has carried a single cup to the table by the window. He
crouches, adjusts. He talks half to Mei, half to himself — gentle,
technical.

                    CHOON
          Not the overhead light — that flattens
          it. You want it from the side. See? Now
          the steam reads. The crema catches.

He nudges the cup a few degrees.

                    CHOON (CONT'D)
          Three-quarter angle. You can see into the
          cup, but you still see the surface. The
          creaminess.

CLICK. He turns the camera around and shows her the screen.

On it: the coffee she makes every single day — but luminous. Warm.
The foam glowing, a curl of steam, the crema like silk.

Mei stares.

                    MEI
          (quiet)
          That's... my coffee?

                    CHOON
          It was always your coffee. Nobody could
          see it.


MONTAGE — MAKING IT VISIBLE

— Choon shooting the croissants, the cakes, Mei's hands on the tamper.

— The two of them at a laptop: the photos going up — a clean social
  post, the café's page filling with warm, glowing images.

— Outside the door, the old chalkboard comes down. A printed STANDEE
  goes up: one gorgeous photo of the flat white. One word: OPEN.

— Mei, nervous, rebuilding the menu — now every item has an image.


EXT. SECONDS — DAY

The same street. The same current of people. The SAME YOUNG COUPLE
from the opening slows at the standee.

This time they stop. They look at the photo. They look at each other.

They come in.


INT. SECONDS — DAY — LATER

Busier now. Three tables full, a small queue at the counter. Mei
works the machine, flushed, alive.

She takes the letter from beside the register. Looks at it. Opens a
drawer and slips it inside. Not today.

Choon sits at his usual table, camera packed away, just drinking his
coffee, watching it work. Mei brings him a fresh cup and sets it down.

                    MEI
          What do I owe you.

                    CHOON
          (lifting the cup)
          You make the best coffee on the street.
          (he sips)
          I just took the photo.

He nods toward the window — a new customer is photographing her own
cup before she drinks it, the way people do now.

                    CHOON (CONT'D)
          Now everybody can see it.

Mei follows his gaze to the customer's phone screen — her coffee,
glowing, about to go online.

Off Mei's face — relief, and something close to pride.


TITLE CARD:

                         GOOD LIGHT


FADE OUT.


END.
